Water Supply Engineering

  1. Introduction
  • Importance Of Water

Water is one of the five most crucial elements for human existence, alongside air, food, heat, and light. Next to air, water is the most essential for human life. An adequate supply of water, both in quantity and quality, is vital for human survival.

Nutrient Importance: Water is perhaps the most important nutrient in our diets. An adult needs to drink about 2 liters (8 glasses) of water daily to replenish the water lost through the skin, respiratory tract, and urine.

Usable Water: Only 0.3% of the Earth’s water is usable by humans. The rest is saltwater, ice, or water vapor.

Freshwater Sources: Freshwater is found in rivers, lakes, and underground sources. However, not all freshwater is suitable for drinking.

Health Impact: Contaminated water can cause serious diseases like cholera, typhoid, and hepatitis. Industrial waste and agricultural chemicals can also make water toxic.

  • Definition of Types of Water
  1. Pure Water: Contains only hydrogen and oxygen (H₂O). It is colorless, tasteless, and odorless. Pure water is not found in nature as it dissolves gases and minerals from the atmosphere.
  2. Impure Water: Contains minerals and other substances. It is used for drinking but must be within acceptable limits.
  3. Potable Water: Safe for drinking, with a pleasant taste and suitable for domestic use.
  4. Wholesome Water: Contains beneficial minerals and is free from harmful impurities.

  • Colorless and sparkling.

  • Non-corrosive to pipes.
  • Contains dissolved oxygen and is free from carbonic acid.
  1. Polluted Water: Contains excess impurities like minerals, salts, gases, and microorganisms. Not fit for drinking.
  2. Contaminated Water: Contains harmful substances like bacteria, viruses, and heavy metals that cause diseases.
  • Historical Development Of Water Supply System

Early Settlements: Developed near rivers for easy access to water. As populations grew, the demand for water increased, leading to pollution and depletion of water sources.

Water Supply Systems: Developed to provide clean, accessible water. In Nepal, traditional sources included hiti, kuwa, dhunge dhara, and wells.

Modern Systems: The first piped water supply in Nepal was the Bir Dhara system in 1895. The Department of Water Supply and Sewerage (DWSS) was established in 1972 to provide nationwide service.

  • Objectives of Water Supply System

The primary objective of a water supply system is to provide water from the best available source, ensuring it is of good physical quality, free from unpleasant taste or odor, and safe for health.

  • 1 Provide safe, wholesome water to consumers in adequate quantity efficiently and at low cost.
  • 2 Make water easily accessible to the public.
  • 3 Ensure adequate provisions for emergencies like firefighting, festivals, and meetings.
  • 4 Save time in fetching water.
  • 5 Supply water reliably, with high quality and quantity, effectively and efficiently.

  • Surface Sources Of Water

Water available at the ground/earth surface is termed as a surface source. The quality and quantity of surface water depend on rainfall patterns, climatic, and geological factors. The quality of water varies from its origin until it reaches the oceans. During storms, surface water carries numerous impurities such as organic and suspended matter. Various forms of surface sources of water include:

  1. Ponds:

Ponds are natural or artificial depressions filled with water, primarily from rainfall. They contain many impurities and are not suitable for water supply purposes. The quantity of water in ponds is usually low and is used for washing, animal bathing, etc.

 

  1. Lakes:

Lakes are large natural depressions or hollows formed in the earth’s surface, filled with water. They are mostly found in mountainous regions. The quantity of water in lakes is generally large, depending on their size, catchment area, annual rainfall, ground porosity, and geological formation. The quality of lake water depends on the characteristics of the catchment area. Lakes in uninhabited upland hilly areas have relatively good quality water, though small lakes with still water may require high degrees of treatment due to algae, weeds, and other vegetation.

  1. Streams:

Streams are natural channels that carry surface runoff from small catchments. They may be perennial or non-perennial. The quality of water in streams is generally good in high altitudes, except during the first runoff. Streams may contain impurities and require some treatment.

  1. Rivers:

Rivers are natural channels that carry surface runoff from their catchment or drainage area. They can be perennial (water available throughout the year) or non-perennial (water not available throughout the year). Perennial rivers are fed by rains and melting snow. River water quality is good near the point of origin in the mountains but deteriorates as the river approaches plains, picking up suspended matter, clay, and silt, making the water muddy and requiring treatment.

  1. Storage/Impounding Reservoirs

Storage or impounding reservoirs are artificial lakes created by constructing a dam or barrier across a river. They store excess water during high flow periods for use during low flow periods. The quality of water in storage reservoirs depends on the quality of the river water and needs proper analysis and treatment before supply. Construction of impounding reservoirs is not feasible when the average annual flow is lower than the average demand or when the river’s flow rate in the dry season exceeds the demand.

  • Ground/Sub-Surface Sources of Water

Water existing below the ground surface is termed as a ground source. Groundwater acquires its chemical characteristics from surface water that percolates into the ground. The quality of groundwater is generally good due to natural filtration as it percolates and infiltrates deeper strata. Groundwater is not exposed to the atmosphere, making it free from direct contamination and pollution from runoff. Various forms of ground sources of water include:

  1. Springs

Springs occur when an aquifer or underground channel reaches the ground surface, causing water to flow naturally. They are classified into:

  1. Gravity Springs: Water flows naturally due to gravity.
  2. Depression Spring: Formed by the overflowing of the water table where the ground surface intersects it. Flow varies with the water table’s rise and fall.
  3. Contact/Surface Spring: Created by a permeable water-bearing formation overlying a less permeable formation that intersects the ground surface.
  4. Non-Gravity Springs:

Volcanic Springs: Associated with volcanic rocks, often thermal and mineralized.

Fissure Springs: Result from fractures extending deep into the earth’s crust, often thermal and mineralized.

  1. Artesian Spring: Release of groundwater under pressure from a confined aquifer through an opening in the confining bed.
  2. Non-Gravity Springs: Water does not flow naturally and may require pumping.
  3. Infiltration Galleries

Infiltration galleries are horizontal tunnels or pipes laid below the water table to collect groundwater.

  1. Infiltration Wells

Infiltration wells are vertical wells that collect groundwater through infiltration.

  1. Wells and Tube Wells

A tube well is a long pipe sunk into the ground to access water-bearing strata. They have smaller diameters compared to open wells and can be classified into:

  1. Open/Dug/Draw Wells: Shallow and deep wells that collect groundwater. Large diameters, low yields, shallow depths (1-10 m diameter, 2-20 m depth). Construction: Built with brick, stone masonry, or precast rings.

Types:

  1. Shallow Open Wells: Rest in the top water-bearing strata, drawing water from the surroundings. Risk of contamination is high.
  2. Deep Open Wells: Penetrate deeper into the ground, accessing water from deeper strata.
  3. Tube Wells: A tube well is a long pipe sunk into the ground to access water-bearing strata. They have smaller diameters compared to open wells and can be classified into:

 Type:

  1. Strainer Type Tube Well:
  2. Most common type.
  3. Uses a combination of strainer and blind pipes.
  4. Strainer pipes rest against water-bearing strata, while blind pipes rest against impervious strata.
  5. Cavity Type Tube Well:
  6. Pipe rests on a strong stratum.
  7. Initially pumps sandy water, forming a cavity at the bottom.
  8. Over time, clear water is obtained.
  9. Slotted Type Tube Well:
  10. Pipe is slotted at one end and plain at the other.
  11. Surrounded by a mixture of gravel and coarse sand to prevent fine sand from entering.
  12. Perforated Type Tube Well:
  13. Shallow depth, used for short durations.
  14. Pipes covered with jute ropes act as strainers.
  15. Artesian Wells

An artesian well is a pump-less water source where groundwater flows automatically under pressure from a confined aquifer. They are typically found in valleys where aquifers are inclined towards the valley, causing water to flow under pressure.

  1. Types of Aquifers

An aquifer is a geological formation that contains sufficient permeable material to store and transmit water. Common terms include groundwater reservoir and water-bearing formation.

  1. Confined Aquifer: Groundwater is confined under pressure greater than atmospheric pressure by overlying impermeable strata, also known as artesian or pressure aquifer.
  2. Unconfined Aquifer: The water table forms the upper surface of the zone of saturation.
  3. Aquiclude and Aquifuge
  4. Aquiclude: An impermeable body of rock or sediment that acts as a barrier to water flow (e.g., clay layer).
  5. Aquifuge: An impermeable body of rock with no interconnected openings, neither absorbing nor transmitting water (e.g., granite bed).

  • Selection of Water Source

When selecting a water source for a town or city, several factors must be considered to ensure reliability, sustainability, safety, and cost-effectiveness. Here are the key factors:

  1. Quality of Water: The source should provide safe, wholesome water free from pollution and undesirable impurities. The fewer impurities, the easier and cheaper it is to treat the water.
  2. Quantity of Water: The source must supply enough water to meet the city’s demands throughout the design period, even during dry seasons. The safe yield should be adequate to meet the desired demand year-round.
  3. Location: Ideally, the water source should be close to the community and situated in an elevated area to reduce the length of pipes and allow gravity flow, minimizing pumping costs. Surface sources are often preferred over groundwater to avoid environmental impacts like lowering the water table.
  4. Cost of Water Supply Project: The overall cost should be minimized to provide affordable water to consumers. Costs depend on the source’s location, elevation, water quality, topography, and distance from the community.

The design period is the number of years a water supply project is planned to serve, considering future demand. It should be neither too long (financially burdensome) nor too short (uneconomical).

  1. QUALITY OF WATER

Pure water is rarely found in nature. Even rainwater, which is initially pure, picks up gases, dust, and other substances as it passes through the atmosphere. As it reaches the earth’s surface, it collects organic and suspended matter. Groundwater, which percolates through the soil, dissolves various salts, minerals, and organic and inorganic matter. Thus, water from both surface and ground sources contains impurities.

  • Impurities in Water, Their Classification, and Effects

Impurities in water can be classified in two ways:

  1. Based on Properties or Characteristics:
  • Physical Impurities: Affect color, odor, taste, and turbidity. Examples include organic matter, minerals, and microorganisms.
  • Chemical Impurities: Affect pH, solids, salts, hardness, alkalinity, chloride, and nitrogen. These can cause various diseases.
  • Bacteriological Impurities: Include pathogenic (e.g., Salmonella) and non-pathogenic (e.g., E. coli) microorganisms, which can cause diseases.
  1. Based on State of Presence/Occurrences:
  • Suspended Impurities: Solid particles causing turbidity, such as silt, clay, algae, fungi, and organic and inorganic matter. These can be removed by settling or filtration.
  • Dissolved Impurities: Organic compounds, inorganic salts, and gases dissolved in water. Examples include salts of calcium and magnesium, iron oxide, and gases like O₂ and CO₂. These can be removed by precipitation, adsorption, or distillation.
  • Colloidal Impurities: Very small particles that cannot be removed by ordinary filters. They remain in continuous motion due to their electrical charge and are often associated with bacteria. These can be removed by coagulation and sedimentation followed by filtration.

  • Hardness and Alkalinity

Hardness is a characteristic of water that prevents the formation of sufficient lather or foam with soap. It is caused by the presence of bicarbonates, sulphates, chlorides, and nitrates of calcium and magnesium. Excessive hardness can interfere with soap usage, boiler operations, dyeing processes, and can cause corrosion in pipes and affect the taste of food.

  • Types of Hardness
  1. Temporary or Carbonate Hardness (CH): Caused by bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium. Also known as temporary hardness. Can be removed by boiling the water or adding lime.
  2. Permanent or Non-carbonate Hardness (NCH): Caused by sulphates, chlorides, and nitrates of calcium and magnesium. Also known as permanent hardness. Cannot be removed by simple boiling.

Hardness is expressed in terms of equivalent amount of CaCO₃.

  • Types of Alkalinity

Alkalinity is caused by hydroxides, carbonates, and bicarbonates, with most natural alkalinity due to bicarbonates. It can be classified as:

  1. Hydroxide Alkalinity (Caustic Alkalinity): Caused by hydroxides.
  2. Carbonate Alkalinity: Caused by carbonates.
  3. Bicarbonate Alkalinity: Caused by bicarbonates.

☺Total alkalinity (TA) is the sum of carbonate alkalinity (CA) and bicarbonate alkalinity (BCA).

  • Water-Related Diseases

Safe water is essential to control many diseases. Water-related diseases are classified into four categories:

  • 41 Water-Borne Diseases

These diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms transmitted through contaminated water. Infection can occur during bathing, washing, drinking, food preparation, or consumption of contaminated food. Common water-borne diseases include:

Cholera: Caused by the bacterium Vibrio cholerae, leading to severe vomiting and watery stools.

Typhoid Fever: Caused by Salmonella bacteria, characterized by fever, headache, slow heartbeat, and malaise.

Diarrhea: An intestinal disorder caused by viruses, bacteria, or parasites, leading to frequent and fluid fecal evacuations.

Dysentery: An infectious disease causing inflammation and ulceration of the bowels, resulting in mucous and hemorrhagic diarrhea.

  • 42 Water-Washed Diseases

These diseases are transmitted due to lack of cleanliness, poor sanitation, and inadequate water usage. They depend on the quantity of water used rather than its quality. Common water-washed diseases include:

 

Scabies: A contagious skin disease caused by the itch mite Sarcoptes scabiei.

Trachoma: A chronic eye disease caused by Chlamydia trachomatis, leading to inflammation and potential blindness.

Bacillary Dysentery (Shigellosis): An acute intestinal infection caused by Shigella bacteria, common among children.

  • 43 Water-Based Diseases

These diseases involve pathogens that spend part of their life cycle in water or aquatic animals. They are also known as water contact diseases. Examples include:

Schistosomiasis: Caused by parasitic flukes of the genus Schistosoma, transmitted through feces-contaminated water or snails.

Guinea Worm Disease: Caused by the parasitic worm Dracunculus medinensis, transmitted through contaminated water.

  • 44 Water Vector Diseases

These diseases are spread by insects that breed in or near water. The transmission involves a host, a parasite, and a vector (insect, fly, or mosquito). Examples include:

Malaria: Caused by Plasmodium protozoa, transmitted by the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito.

Onchocerciasis (River Blindness): Caused by filarial worms of the genus Onchocerca, transmitted by black flies.

Dengue: An eruptive fever caused by a virus, transmitted by mosquito bites.

  • 1 Protect food from flies.

  1. Ensure Proper Hygiene:
  • 1 Drink only filtered water.
  • 2 Wash hands properly before eating.
  • 3 Eat cooked, warm foods.
  • 4 Keep fingernails short and clean.
  • 5 Use proper toilets for defecation.
  • 6 Wash food before cooking and cook at high temperatures to kill harmful bacteria.
  • 7 Dispose of animal and organic wastes properly to avoid flies.
  • 8 Dispose of infant and toddler feces properly.
  • 9 Avoid consuming foods, fruit juices, and milkshakes from roadside vendors.
  • 10 Keep foods and beverages covered.
  • 11 Avoid drinking water at parks and recreational places; carry bottled water or your own water.
  • 12 Wash hands frequently, especially before preparing food, before eating, after using the toilet, changing diapers, using a handkerchief, changing soiled clothes or beddings, caring for people with water-borne illnesses, and after playing with pets and animals.
  • Examination of Water

Water examination, or water analysis, is conducted to identify water quality and the presence of impurities. The purposes of water analysis include:

  1. Ascertaining the quality of water and the quantity of various impurities.
  2. Verifying if treated water meets standards.
  3. Identifying the dose of chlorine, coagulant, etc., in treatment plants.
  4. Prescribing the degree of treatment required for the desired water quality.
  • Sampling Techniques
  • Use fully cleaned bottles, buckets, or jerricans for physical tests; containers should be greater than 2 liters for biological and chemical tests.
  • Rinse containers before sampling.
  • Shake the sample well before testing to ensure air remains in the container.
  • Let the tap run for 2-3 minutes to avoid stagnant water during sample collection from distribution lines.
  • When sampling from a well with a hand pump, pump water for about 5 minutes before collecting it in a clean, sterilized container. If there is no hand pump, take the water directly from the well.
  • For river, reservoir, or lake samples, take water from 40-50 cm below the surface, not too far or near the draw-off site.
  • Test samples within 1 hour of collection for microbiological tests; if not possible, keep the sample in an ice chest or cooler for up to 24 hours.
  • Physical Examination of Water
  • Temperature: Measured by ordinary thermometers. Ideal for public water supply is between 10°C to 15.6°C. Temperatures above 25°C are undesirable, and above 35°C are unfit for public supply.
  • Colour: Pure water is colorless. Colour in water is measured by its ability to absorb light, using a tintometer. Drinking water should not have a colour greater than 5 ppm on the platinum-cobalt scale.

  1. INTAKES

An intake is a device or structure installed in a water source to withdraw water and discharge it into an intake conduit, then to the treatment plant. It consists of openings, gratings or strainers, valves, operating devices, pumps, and a structure or housing to support the intake conduit.

  • Site Selection of an Intake

Key considerations for selecting an intake site include:

  1. Proximity to the treatment plant to reduce conveyance costs.
  2. High water quality to reduce treatment costs.
  3. Avoiding locations near wastewater disposal points.
  4. Ensuring supply during worst conditions by fetching water from deeper portions of the river.
  5. Allowing for future extensions and additions.
  6. Accessibility during floods.
  7. Avoiding meandering sections of rivers; prefer concave or outer banks.
  8. Geologically stable locations free from erosion, silting, scouring, and heavy currents.
  9. Good road connectivity.
  10. Considering natural factors like seasonal variations, winds, currents, and climate.

  • Contamination Prevention

Springs are susceptible to contamination by surface water, especially during rainstorms. To prevent this:

  1. Construct a U-shaped surface drainage diversion ditch or an earth berm at least 15 meters uphill from the spring to divert surface runoff.
  2. Fence an area at least 30 meters in all directions around the spring box to prevent contamination by livestock and unaware people.
  3. Plant vegetation around the spring source to maintain discharge.
  • General Requirements for Spring Intake Location
  • Proximity to Source: Should be as close to the source as possible.
  • Elevation: Should be above populated or farming areas.
  • Accessibility: Should be above footpaths, cattle watering, and washing places.
  • Drainage: Should allow easy drainage of surface runoff during rain.
  • Accessibility: Should not be easily accessible to people and livestock.
  • Water Logging: Should not allow water logging near the intake.
  • Construction Considerations
  • Backup Pressure Prevention: Construct the collection chamber away from the source with a head of about 4 to 5 meters to allow free flow.
  • Avoid Stone Soiling: Prevent leakage by avoiding stone soiling below the floor.
  • Light Structure: Avoid heavy intake structures to prevent settlement.
  • Valve Box Space: Provide adequate space in the valve box for easy repair and maintenance.

  • Inlet Pipe Protection: Cover the inlet pipe with stone soiling and impervious material upstream to prevent entry of suspended particles.
  • Animal Access Restriction: Restrict animal access within at least 30 meters of the intake to avoid contamination.
  • Surface Runoff Drainage: Ensure easy drainage of surface runoff by providing drains around the intake area.
  • Yield Consideration: Springs with a yield of less than 0.05 liters per second should not be tapped for gravity flow schemes.
  1. TREATMENT OF WATER

Water treatment involves processes to make water suitable for its intended purpose by removing or reducing impurities. The goal is to produce water that is hygienically safe, aesthetically attractive, and palatable.

  • Objectives of Water Treatment
  • Meet Standards: Ensure water meets requisite standards.
  • Reduce Impurities: Lower impurities to acceptable levels.
  • Potability: Make water safe for drinking.
  • Disinfection: Kill disease-causing organisms.
  • Corrosion Minimization: Reduce water’s corrosive nature to protect pipes.
  • Water Treatment Processes

The choice and arrangement of processes depend on factors like raw water quality, desired standards, source type, impurity types, available funds, and supply purpose. Common processes include:

  • Screening: Removes large suspended and floating matters.
  • Plain Sedimentation: Separates coarse or heavier suspended particles.
  • Sedimentation with Coagulation: Removes electrically charged finer particles, especially colloidal.
  • Filtration: Removes bacteria, finer particles, and colloidal matter.
  • Disinfection: Kills pathogenic microorganisms, commonly using chlorination.
  • Aeration: Removes color, odor, taste, and dissolved gases.
  • Softening: Removes hardness from water.
  • De-chlorination: Removes excess chlorine.
  • Miscellaneous Treatments: Removes iron, manganese, and other harmful substances.

Screening is the process of passing water through screens to remove large suspended matters like sticks, branches, leaves, dead animals, and debris. Screens have uniform-sized openings and can be circular or rectangular. The screening element can be made of parallel bars, rods, wires, grating, wire mesh, or perforated plates.

  • Ideal Sedimentation Tank

An ideal sedimentation tank ensures that the time taken by water particles to move from the inlet to the outlet is equal to the time taken by suspended particles to settle. It is divided into four zones:

  1. Inlet Zone: Dissipates momentum and establishes uniform flow.
  2. Settling Zone: Where quiescent settling occurs.
  3. Outlet Zone: Flow converges upwards to decanting weirs or launders.
  4. Sludge Zone: Collects settled material and moves it towards sludge hoppers for withdrawal.
  • Design of Horizontal Flow Sedimentation Tank
    1. Design assumptions include:
  1. Particles settle in the same way as in a quiescent tank of equal depth.
  2. Flow of water is steady and velocity is uniform in the settling zone.

  4. Particles reaching the sludge zone are removed and do not rise.

Sedimentation tanks are designed to remove suspended particles from water by allowing them to settle. They can be classified into two main types:

Flocculation: The process of forming flocs from the coagulated particles, which then settle due to their increased weight.

More complete and rapid removal of suspended particles and colloidal matter.

  • Filtration

Filtration is a crucial step in modern water treatment plants, often used to remove small flocs or precipitant particles not eliminated by sedimentation. It involves passing water through a stationary bed of granular medium, typically sand supported by gravel, to retain impurities.

 

  • Key Principles of Filtration
  1. Mechanical Straining: Suspended impurities larger than the voids between sand grains are trapped and removed.
  2. Sedimentation and Adsorption: Voids between sand grains act as mini sedimentation tanks, settling suspended particles. A gelatinous coating on sand grains helps remove finer particles and bacteria.
  3. Biological Metabolism: Bacteria in the sand grains break down organic impurities into harmless compounds, forming a layer called ‘dirty skin’ that aids in further filtration.
  4. Electrolytic Action: Opposite electric charges between filter media and particles neutralize each other, changing the chemical characteristics of water.

  • Chlorination Process

When chlorine is added to water, it undergoes hydrolysis to form hypochlorous acid (HOCl) and hypochlorite ions (OCl⁻). HOCl is a more effective disinfectant than OCl⁻. The effectiveness of chlorine depends on the pH of the water, with HOCl being more prevalent at pH levels between 5 and 7.

  • Chlorine Demand and Residual Chlorine

Chlorine Demand: The amount of chlorine consumed by organic and inorganic matter in water.

Residual Chlorine: The amount of chlorine remaining in water after the chlorine demand is met, providing ongoing disinfection.

  • Dosage and Contact Period

Dosage: The amount of chlorine added to water, typically enough to leave about 0.2 mg/L of residual chlorine after a contact period of 10 minutes.

Contact Period: The time required for chlorine to effectively kill microorganisms, usually around 10 minutes.

  • Types of Chlorine Application
  • Bleaching Powder (Calcium Hypochlorite): Used for small-scale applications like swimming pools. It is unstable and requires careful storage.
  • Chloramines: Formed by adding ammonia to water before chlorine. Less effective than free chlorine but provides longer-lasting disinfection.
  • Chlorine Gas or Liquid Chlorine: Used for large-scale water treatment.
  • Chlorine Dioxide: Effective but more complex to use and handle.
  • Forms of Chlorination

Chlorination is a key method for disinfecting water, involving the addition of chlorine to kill or neutralize harmful microorganisms.

  • Simple or Plain Chlorination: Only chlorine is added to relatively clear raw water (0.5 to 1 ppm).
  • Pre-chlorination: Chlorine is added before any treatment to control algae, reduce bacterial load, and improve filtration (0.1 to 0.5 ppm residual chlorine).
  • Post-chlorination: Chlorine is added after all treatments, just before distribution (0.1 to 0.2 ppm).
  • Multiple or Double Chlorination: Chlorine is added at multiple points, especially for highly contaminated water.
  • Break Point Chlorination: Chlorine is added until all chlorine demand is met, achieving free chlorine residual. Effective for removing taste, odor, and ensuring bactericidal effect.
  • Super Chlorination: Chlorine is added beyond the break point, used during epidemics (2 to 3 mg/L).
  • De-chlorination: Excess chlorine is removed to avoid taste issues, using methods like aeration or adding chemicals.
  • Factors Affecting Chlorination Efficiency
  • Turbidity: High turbidity can protect microorganisms from chlorine.
  • Metallic Compounds: Metals like iron and manganese react with chlorine.
  • Ammonia Compounds: Form less effective chloramines.
  • pH Value: Chlorine is more effective at lower pH levels.
  • Temperature: Higher temperatures increase disinfection rate.
  • Contact Time: Longer contact time improves effectiveness.
  • Microorganism Type and Concentration: Different microorganisms have varying resistance.

  • Methods of Aeration

Aeration is the process of bringing water into intimate contact with air to remove dissolved gases, oxidize dissolved metals, and improve water quality. Here are the main methods:

  1. Free Fall or Gravity Aerators

Cascade Aerator: Water flows over a series of steps, creating thin sheets that increase air contact.

Inclined Apron or Riffle Plate Aerators: Water flows down an inclined surface, breaking into sheets for aeration.

Tray Aerator: Water falls through a series of trays, each intercepting the flow to enhance aeration.

Gravel Packed Bed Aerator: Water percolates through beds of coke, limestone, or anthracite, with air blown from below.

Trickling Bed Aerator: Similar to gravel bed aerators, using trays filled with coke or stone to aerate water.

  1. Spray Aerator

Water is sprayed through nozzles into the air, breaking into droplets. This method is efficient for removing CO₂ and H₂S.

  1. Diffuse Air Aerator

Compressed air is blown through perforated pipes at the bottom of a tank, creating bubbles that rise and aerate the water.

  1. Mechanical Aeration

A propeller connected to a motor mixes air into the water, achieving aeration through mechanical agitation.

  3. RESERVOIR AND DISTRIBUTION

Water distribution involves delivering purified water to consumers through a network of pipes, reservoirs, pumps, valves, and meters. The goal is to provide safe, adequate, and reliable water supply while minimizing losses.

  • Requirements of a Good Distribution System
  • Deliver adequate water at required pressure.
  • Maintain water quality.
  • Easy to operate and maintain.
  • Minimize water loss.
  • Safe against pipe bursts.
  • Capable of meeting emergency needs like firefighting.
  • Systems of Supply
  1. Continuous Supply: Water is available 24/7.

Advantages: No need for private storage, fresh water always available, useful for firefighting.

Disadvantages: Potential for water wastage, requires metering to control usage, interruptions during maintenance.

  1. Intermittent Supply: Water is supplied at fixed hours.

Advantages: Useful when water availability is limited, less wastage during non-supply hours.

Disadvantages: Requires private storage, inconvenient for consumers, potential for contamination during non-supply hours, requires more valves and larger pipes.

  • Clear Water Reservoirs
  • Purpose: Store treated water before distribution.
  • Capacity: Typically 14 to 16 hours of average daily flow.
  • Construction: Often half below ground and half above, with multiple compartments for maintenance.
  • Service/Distribution Reservoirs
  • Purpose: Provide storage to meet demand fluctuations, firefighting, emergencies, and stabilize pressure.
  • Location: Near the area served to minimize head loss and save on pipeline costs.
  • Construction: Can be made of stone masonry, brick masonry, plain cement concrete, or reinforced concrete. Always covered to avoid contamination and prevent algal growth. Equipped with mosquito-proof ventilation, manholes, access ladders, scour valves, overflow valves, and water level indicators.
  • Types of Service Reservoirs
  • Surface Reservoirs: Constructed at or below ground level. Used to lift water to elevated reservoirs or supply directly if located at higher elevations.
  • Elevated Reservoirs: Also known as overhead tanks, constructed above ground. Common shapes include rectangular, circular, elliptical, spherical, and egg-shaped.
  • Standpipes: Vertical cylindrical tanks made of steel or RCC, used to increase pressure in the distribution system.
  • Storage Capacity of Service Reservoirs
  • Balancing Reserve: To meet fluctuating demand with constant pumping. Determined using hydrograph or mass curve methods.
  • Breakdown Storage: For emergencies like pump failures or repairs. Typically 25% of total capacity or 1.5 to 2 times the average hourly supply.
  • Fire Storage: To provide water for firefighting.

  • Layout of Distribution System

Water distribution systems can be laid out in various configurations to efficiently deliver water to consumers. Here are the main types:

  1. Dead-End or Tree System: Main pipeline runs through the center, with sub-mains branching off and further dividing into smaller branches.

Advantages: Simple design, easy to calculate discharge and pressure, economical.

Disadvantages: Sediment accumulation at dead ends, less effective pressure maintenance, low water availability for firefighting.

  1. Grid-Iron or Reticulation System: Main pipeline runs through the center, with sub-mains and branches interconnected in a grid pattern.

Advantages: No sediment stagnation, minimal head loss, small area affected during repairs, better firefighting water availability.

Disadvantages: Requires more cutoff valves and longer pipes, complex design and calculations.

  1. Circular or Ring System: Main pipeline forms a closed ring around the area, with sub-mains running inside.

Advantages: Water can be supplied from multiple directions, large water availability for firefighting.

Disadvantages: Longer pipe lengths, similar merits and demerits as the grid-iron system.

  1. Radial System: Area is divided into zones, each with a central distribution reservoir. Water flows radially from the center.

Advantages: Ensures high pressure, quick and efficient distribution.

Disadvantages: Requires more reservoirs, similar merits and demerits as the grid-iron system.

  1. CONVEYANCE OF WATER

Water is transported from the source to the treatment plant and then to consumers. This can be done through gravity conduits or pressure conduits.

  1. Gravity Conduits: Water flows under gravity, with the hydraulic grade line (HGL) coinciding with the water surface.

Types: Open channels, flumes, aqueducts.

  1. Pressure Conduits: Water flows under pressure above atmospheric pressure, allowing for flexible routing. HGL generally lies above the conduit.
  • Pipe Materials

Pipes used in water conveyance and distribution are made from various materials, each with specific properties.

  • Requirements of Good Pipe Material
  • Adequate structural strength.
  • Impervious, corrosion-resistant, durable.
  • Cost-effective and readily available.
  • Low friction coefficient for high hydraulic efficiency.
  • Resistant to abrasion.
  • Lightweight for easy handling.
  • Easy to join, repair, and maintain.
  • Environmentally friendly.
  • Types of Pipe Materials
  1. Cast Iron (CI) Pipes

Advantages: Durable, corrosion-resistant, strong.

Disadvantages: Heavy, brittle, expensive, metallic taste in water.

  1. Galvanized Iron (GI) Pipes

Advantages: Cheap, lightweight, easy to handle and join.

Disadvantages: Affected by acidic/alkaline water, less durable.

  1. Steel Pipes

Advantages: Strong, flexible, light, can resist high pressure.

Disadvantages: High maintenance cost, prone to rust. not suitable for distribution pipes, may deform under external forces.

  1. Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC) Pipes

Advantages: Cheap, durable, flexible, corrosion-resistant, good electric insulators, lightweight.

Disadvantages: High coefficient of expansion, difficult to obtain uniform composition, less heat-resistant, some types impart taste to water.

  1. Polypropylene Random Copolymer (PPR) Pipes

Advantages: Good hydraulic efficiency, lightweight, high heat resistance, eco-friendly, durable, good chemical resistance, no calcification, long life expectancy.

Disadvantages: Requires fusion-welding tool for joining and repairing.

  1. Concrete Pipes

Advantages: Durable, can be cast on-site, low maintenance cost, smooth inside surface, no rusting.

Disadvantages: Heavy, difficult to transport and repair, high initial cost, affected by acids and salts.

  • Types of Pipe Joints
  1. Socket and Spigot Joint: Commonly used for CI and DI pipes. Spigot end of one pipe fits into the socket end of another, sealed with jute or hemp and molten lead.

Advantages: Strong, durable, watertight.

  1. Flanged Joint: Used for CI, steel, and GI pipes. Flanges of both pipes are bolted together with a gasket in between.

Advantages: Easy to assemble and disassemble, suitable for temporary works.

  1. Expansion Joint: Allows for expansion and contraction due to temperature changes. Socket end flanged with a cast iron ring, sealed with a rubber gasket.

Advantages: Maintains watertightness under temperature stress.

  1. Collar Joint: Used for concrete pipes. Ends of pipes are joined with a collar and sealed with cement mortar.

Advantages: Simple, effective for concrete pipes.

  1. Screwed and Socket Joint: Commonly used for GI and small diameter steel pipes. Ends of pipes have screw threads, joined with a socket.

Advantages: Easy to join and repair.

  • Preparation of Detailed Maps: Survey and prepare maps showing pipeline alignment, including roads, sewer lines, existing water pipes, telephone lines, and electric lines.
  • Locating Alignment on Ground: Mark the alignment with pegs at intervals of 30 m on straight sections and 7.5 to 15 m on curves.
  • Pipe Laying with Respect to Ground Level: Transmission mains may be laid above ground with suitable supports, while distribution pipes are typically laid below ground.

  • Lowering Pipes: Carefully transport and lower pipes into the trench, ensuring protective coatings and pipe ends are not damaged.
  • Joining Pipes: Select and join pipes using appropriate joints. Fit valves at proper locations.
  • Testing Pipes: Conduct leakage tests on sections of about 500 m. Pump water at twice the normal pressure and check for leaks after 24 hours.

  1. VALVES AND FITTINGS

Valves control flow, maintain pressure, prevent backflow, and release air from pipelines. Common types include:

  • Sluice or Gate Valve: Regulates flow by raising or lowering a disc or gate.
  • Reflux (Check) Valve: Allows water to flow in one direction only, preventing backflow.
  • Safety or Pressure Relief Valve: Releases excess pressure to prevent pipe bursts.
  • Air Relief Valve: Releases trapped air from pipelines.
  • Drain or Scour Valve: Removes sediment and debris from pipelines.
  • Break Pressure Tank (BPT)

A BPT is a small tank that reduces excessive hydrostatic pressure in pipelines, preventing pipe bursts. It establishes a new static water level and is equipped with various valves for inlet, outlet, overflow, and washout.
