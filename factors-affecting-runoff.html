Hydrology & Engineering

Factors Affecting Runoff from Catchment Areas

A simplified technical bulletin explaining why rainwater flows over land surfaces.

💧 Definition: What is Runoff?

When rain falls, some of it soaks into the ground (infiltration). When the ground cannot absorb any more water, the extra water flows over the surface. This flowing water is called runoff.

1. Rainfall Characteristics

The type of rain is the most immediate driver of runoff.

🌧️ Rainfall Intensity (How hard it rains)

  • Heavy Rain: If rain falls faster than the soil can drink it up, it flows over the top. This causes high runoff.
  • Gentle Rain: If rain falls slowly, the soil has time to absorb it. This produces low runoff.

⏱️ Rainfall Duration (How long it rains)

  • Short Duration: A quick burst may be absorbed entirely by dry soil.
  • Long Duration: If it rains for a long time, the soil becomes full (saturated) like a wet sponge. Once full, all additional rain becomes runoff.

2. Soil Characteristics

The soil acts as a natural sponge. Its condition determines how much water stays and how much runs away.

Soil Type Characteristics Runoff Potential
Sandy Soil Large gaps between particles; drains fast. ⬇️ Low
Clay Soil Tiny gaps; sticky and hard to penetrate. ⬆️ High
Wet Soil Already full of water from previous rain. ⬆️ High (Immediate)

3. Topography (Shape of the Land)

⛰️ Slope (Steepness)

  • Steep Land: Gravity pulls water down quickly. It does not have time to soak into the ground, leading to high runoff.
  • Flat Land: Water moves slowly and pools on the surface, giving it plenty of time to soak in.

📐 Catchment Shape

Fan-shaped catchments allow water to reach the river mouth simultaneously, causing sudden floods. Elongated catchments spread the flow out over time.

4. Vegetation (Plants & Trees)

Plants are nature’s speed bumps for water.

  • Forests: Leaves catch rain, roots make holes in the soil, and fallen leaves slow down the water. Forests produce the least amount of runoff.
  • Bare Ground: Without plants, rain hits the soil hard, closing up the soil pores and causing rapid water flow.

5. Land Use (Human Activity)

How humans modify the land is a major factor in modern hydrology.

🏙️ Urban Areas (Cities)

Cities are covered in concrete, roads, and roofs. These are "impervious" surfaces (water cannot go through them). Because water cannot soak in, almost 100% of the rain becomes runoff, often leading to flash floods.

📝 Key Takeaway

Runoff Increases with: Heavy rain, steep slopes, clay soil, wet ground, and urban concrete surfaces.

Runoff Decreases with: Gentle rain, flat land, sandy soil, dry ground, and thick forests.
