Properties of Building Materials

      Physical Properties

      These properties help estimate the quality and condition of a material without applying any external force.

      1. Bulk Density: The ratio of mass to volume, influencing strength, heat, and conductivity.
      2. Porosity: The ratio of void volume to material volume, affecting thermal conductivity, strength, and durability.
      3. Water Absorption: The material’s ability to absorb and retain water.
      4. Hygroscopicity: The ability to absorb water vapor from the air.
      5. Fire Resistance: The ability to withstand fire without changing properties.
      6. Frost Resistance: The ability to resist freezing and thawing.

      Mechanical Properties

      Mechanical properties are determined by applying external forces.

      1. Strength: The material’s resistance to failure under load.
      2. Hardness: The resistance to scratching, determined using the MOHS scale.
      3. Elasticity: The ability to regain shape after deformation.
      4. Plasticity: The ability to undergo permanent deformation without cracking.
      5. Brittleness: The tendency to fail suddenly under stress.
      6. Fatigue: Weakening of material under repeated loads.
      7. Impact Strength: The ability to absorb sudden loads.
      8. Creep: Slow deformation under constant load over time.
      9. Coefficient of Softening: The effect of water absorption on strength.

      Chemical Properties

      1. Chemical Resistance: The ability to resist chemical effects like acids and salts.
      2. Corrosion Resistance: The resistance to rust formation in metals.

      Thermal Properties

      1. Thermal Capacity: The ability to absorb heat.
      2. Thermal Conductivity: The rate of heat transfer.
      3. Thermal Resistivity: The ability to resist heat conduction.
      4. Specific Heat: The heat required to raise the temperature of a material.

STONE

      Classification of Stones

      A. Based on Formation / Geological Classification

        1. Igneous Rocks

        Rocks formed by the cooling of magma are called igneous rocks. These are stronger than other types of rocks and have a glossy and fused texture.

        Based on the location of cooling:
            Intrusive Rock: If the cooling of magma occurs below the Earth's surface.
            • Plutonic Rock: Magma cools at great depths (3-8 km). E.g.: Granite, Pegmatite.
            • Hypabyssal Rock: Forms at 2-3 km depth. E.g.: Dolerite, Porphyry.
            Extrusive Rock: If magma cools at the surface, also called volcanic rock. E.g.: Basalt, Andesite, Pumice.

        2. Sedimentary Rocks

        Formed by erosion of igneous rocks, deposited in layers. This process is called petrifaction. E.g.: Sandstone, Limestone, Laterite.

        3. Metamorphic Rocks

        Formed when igneous or sedimentary rocks undergo high pressure and temperature, causing recrystallization.

        Examples:

        • Shale → Slate
        • Limestone → Marble
        • Sandstone → Quartzite

      B. Chemical Classification

        1. Siliceous Rocks: Contain silica. E.g.: Quartzite, Granite.
        2. Argillaceous Rocks: Contain alumina. E.g.: Slate, Laterite.
        3. Calcareous Rocks: Contain lime. E.g.: Marble, Limestone.

      C. Physical Classification

        1. Stratified Rocks: Form in layers.
          • Foliated: Can be split in one direction. E.g.: Limestone, Quartzite.
          • Non-Foliated: Cannot be split. E.g.: Marble.
          2.Unstratified Rocks: Cannot be split into layers. E.g.: Pumice, Granite.

Some Important Stones

Granite:

    - Quartz, feldspar, and mica (Main Composition)
    - Igneous rock
    - Grey, green, brown with mottled appearance
    - Compressive strength: 70 to 130 MN/m²
    - Specific gravity: 2.64
    - Low fire resistance, cracks on heating
    - Used in masonry, flooring, aesthetic columns

Slate:

    -Composed of Alumina, quartz, muscovite, and illite
    -Argillaceous rock
    -Dark blue to grey
    -Compressive strength: 60 to 70 N/m²
    -Specific gravity: 2.8
    -Durable, non-absorbent, used in roofing, electrical boards

Sandstone

    -Composed of Quartz, lime, and silica
    -Grainy structure, available in multiple colors
    -Compressive strength: 35 to 40 MN/m²
    -Specific gravity: 2.65 to 2.95
    -Used in ashlar masonry, random rubble works

Basalt/Trap

    -Silicious rock composed of silica, alumina, and felspar
    -Dark grey color
    -Very hard, tough
    -Compressive strength: 70 to 80 N/m²
    -Specific gravity: 2.96

Limestone

    -Calcareous rock composed of calcite, magnesia, silica, and alumina
    -Dark grey color
    -Compressive strength: 52 MN/m²
    -Specific gravity: 2.6
    -Used in lime manufacturing

Marble

    -Composed of CaCO₃ (Calcite, mica, and quartz)
    -Available in various colors
    -Compressive strength: 50 to 60 MN/m²
    -Specific gravity: 2.72
    -Used for carving, flooring, and decorative works

Laterite

    -Igneous rock undergoing metamorphosis
    -High iron oxide content
    -Porous, cellular structure
    -Reddish brown, dark brown, or yellow in color

Tests of Stones

Crushing Test It is used to test the resistance of the stone to a crushing load. Procedure:
  • Stones are cut into cubes of 40mm.
  • The specimens are soaked in water for 72 hours.
  • With the help of 5mm plywood, load is applied axially at 13.7 KN/min.
  • The crushing strength is determined as the load required to crush the stone divided by its strength.

Hardness Test

It is used to test the resistance of the stone to wear and tear. Procedure:
  • Stone samples of 25mm diameter and height are used.
  • They are placed in Dorry's testing machine with a 1250 gm load.
  • The disc rotates at 28 rpm while sand is sprayed continuously.
  • After 1000 revolutions, the specimen is weighed to calculate hardness loss.

Impact Test

This test determines the stone's resistance to impact load. Procedure:<
  • Sample size: 25mm dia, 25mm height.
  • A 20N steel hammer is dropped at increasing heights.
  • The number of blows before breaking is noted as the toughness index.

Acid Test

Used to determine the stone's weather resistance. Procedure:
  • Stone is immersed in 1% sulfuric acid solution.
  • If it deteriorates, the stone is unsuitable for corrosive environments.

Water Absorption Test

Determines the amount of water absorbed by the stone. Procedure:
  • A cube of 0.5N is prepared and weighed (W1).
  • It is placed in water for 24 hours, then weighed again (W2).
  • Water absorption (%) = ((W2 - W1) / W1) * 100

Smith Test

Used to detect dirty materials in aggregates. Procedure:
  • Stone sample is broken and immersed in water.
  • If water turns dirty, impurities are present.

Freezing Thawing Test

Determines stone's unsoundness. Procedure:
  • Stone is immersed in water.
  • Frozen at -20°C for 24 hours.
  • Thawed at room temperature and cycle repeated.

Characteristics of Good Stones

Good stones should have:
  • High compressive strength: Required for structural applications.
  • Good appearance: Uniform color and smooth surface.
  • High weight: Dense stones are more durable.
  • Low water absorption: To prevent deterioration.
  • Fine grain: Enhances strength and durability.
  • Fire resistance: Stones should withstand high temperatures.
  • Electrical resistance: Important in electrically sensitive areas.

Quarrying, Dressing, and Seasoning

  • Quarrying: Extracting stone from its natural deposit using excavation, blasting, etc.
  • Dressing: Shaping and sizing the stone immediately after quarrying.
  • Seasoning: Removing quarry sap to improve stone strength.
